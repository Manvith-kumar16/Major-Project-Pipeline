## 🧠 Project Overview

### The Problem: Three Critical Gaps in Medical AI

Modern deep learning models have demonstrated near-radiologist-level accuracy in detecting thoracic diseases from chest X-rays. However, deploying such systems in real-world clinical environments exposes **three fundamental and unresolved challenges**:

---

#### 🔒 1. Data Privacy in Healthcare

Patient medical records — including chest X-rays — are governed by strict regulatory frameworks such as **HIPAA** (USA), **GDPR** (Europe), and regional data protection laws. Hospitals **cannot legally or ethically share raw patient data** with external parties for collaborative model training.

> Traditional centralized machine learning demands data aggregation. This is not an option in healthcare.

A model trained on data from a single hospital is inherently biased toward that institution's patient population, scanning hardware, and clinical protocols — leading to poor performance when deployed elsewhere.

---

#### 🌍 2. Domain Shift Across Hospitals

Even when models achieve high accuracy on their training dataset, **they frequently fail when tested on data from a different institution** — a phenomenon known as *domain shift*. This is caused by:

- **Differences in X-ray scanner manufacturers** (e.g., GE vs. Siemens vs. Philips)
- **Variation in imaging protocols** (resolution, exposure, contrast enhancement)
- **Patient population differences** (age, ethnicity, disease prevalence)
- **Label noise** — annotations generated by NLP pipelines vs. board-certified radiologists produce inconsistent ground truth

A model trained on CheXpert (Stanford, USA) may degrade significantly when applied to NIH ChestX-ray14 (USA) or VinDr-CXR (Vietnam), even though all datasets contain chest X-rays of the same diseases.

---

#### 🕵️ 3. Black-Box Nature of Deep Learning

The most accurate deep learning models — DenseNets, ResNets, Vision Transformers — are **inherently opaque**. They do not explain *why* they made a prediction, only *what* they predicted.

This is clinically unacceptable. A radiologist cannot trust a diagnosis that comes without visual evidence. Hospitals face liability risks and regulatory barriers when deploying "black-box" models.

> **Explainability is not optional in medical AI — it is a clinical requirement.**

---

### 💡 Why This Project Matters

This research directly addresses all three challenges through a unified, end-to-end framework:

| Challenge | Our Approach |
|---|---|
| 🔒 Data Privacy | **Federated Learning** — models are trained locally; only weights are shared |
| 🌍 Domain Shift | **Cross-dataset evaluation** on NIH & VinDr after CheXpert training |
| 🕵️ Black-Box AI | **Grad-CAM XAI** — visual heatmaps aligned with radiologist attention |
| 📉 Model Performance | **SE Attention Blocks** — channel-wise recalibration for better feature selection |

## 🎯 Objectives

This project pursues **four tightly integrated research objectives**, each addressing a specific clinical or technical limitation:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                         RESEARCH OBJECTIVES                                 │
│                                                                             │
│  Obj 1: Privacy-Preserving Learning ──────► Federated Learning (FL)         │
│  Obj 2: Cross-Dataset Robustness   ──────► NIH + VinDr Testing              │
│  Obj 3: Interpretable Decisions    ──────► Grad-CAM Explainability          │
│  Obj 4: Performance Improvement    ──────► SE Attention Mechanisms          │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

- 🔐 **Objective 1 — Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning**: Design and implement a federated learning pipeline where each hospital (dataset) trains locally and contributes only model gradients/weights to a central aggregation server, ensuring no patient data ever leaves its source institution.

- 🌐 **Objective 2 — Robust Cross-Dataset Generalization**: Rigorously evaluate models trained on CheXpert against NIH ChestX-ray14 and VinDr-CXR to quantify domain shift and demonstrate that the proposed framework generalizes meaningfully across institutions and geographic contexts.

- 🔬 **Objective 3 — Explainability via Grad-CAM**: Provide visual explanations for every model prediction using **Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM)**, validated on the radiologist-annotated VinDr-CXR dataset to ensure clinical alignment.

- 🧠 **Objective 4 — Attention-Augmented Performance**: Integrate **Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) attention blocks** into DenseNet121 to enable channel-wise feature recalibration, suppressing irrelevant anatomical regions and boosting discriminative capacity.

## 🤖 Model Design

### 7.1 Baseline Model — DenseNet121

**DenseNet121** (Densely Connected Convolutional Network) is selected as the backbone due to its:

- **Dense connectivity pattern**: Each layer receives feature maps from all preceding layers, enabling gradient flow even in deep networks
- **Parameter efficiency**: Feature reuse reduces model size while maintaining representational power
- **Clinical precedent**: DenseNet121 underpins **CheXNet** (Rajpurkar et al., 2017), which achieved radiologist-level pneumonia detection

```
Architecture: DenseNet121
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Input (224 × 224 × 3)
    │
    ▼
Conv2D (7×7, stride=2) + BN + ReLU + MaxPool
    │
    ▼
Dense Block 1 (6 layers)  ──► Transition Layer 1
    │
    ▼
Dense Block 2 (12 layers) ──► Transition Layer 2
    │
    ▼
Dense Block 3 (24 layers) ──► Transition Layer 3
    │
    ▼
Dense Block 4 (16 layers)
    │
    ▼
Global Average Pooling
    │
    ▼
Fully Connected (→ 8 classes) + Sigmoid
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pretrained: ImageNet (1.28M images, 1,000 classes)
Output Activation: Sigmoid (multi-label classification)
Loss Function: Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE)
```

### 7.2 Proposed Model — DenseNet121 + SE Attention

The proposed model augments DenseNet121 with **Squeeze-and-Excitation (SE) Blocks** inserted after each Dense Block.

#### What Do SE Blocks Do?

SE blocks implement **channel-wise attention** — they learn to selectively amplify relevant feature channels (e.g., those encoding lung opacity) while suppressing noise channels (e.g., background tissue).

```
SE Block Operation:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Input Feature Map: F ∈ ℝ^(H × W × C)
         │
         ▼
  SQUEEZE: Global Average Pooling ──► z ∈ ℝ^C
  (Summarize spatial information per channel: z_c = (1/HW) Σ F_c(i,j))
         │
         ▼
  EXCITATION: FC(C → C/r) → ReLU → FC(C/r → C) → Sigmoid
  (Learn channel importance weights: s = σ(W₂ · δ(W₁ · z)))
         │
         ▼
  RECALIBRATION: F̃_c = s_c · F_c
  (Scale each channel by its learned importance)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Reduction Ratio (r): 16   |   Added parameters: < 2% of base
```

**Clinical intuition**: In a chest X-ray, different feature channels activate at different anatomical structures. SE blocks learn that when predicting *Pleural Effusion*, the channels encoding the lower lung margins matter most — and suppress irrelevant channels accordingly.

## ⚙️ Training Strategy

### Phase 1: Transfer Learning

```
Objective: Rapidly adapt ImageNet weights to the chest X-ray domain

  DenseNet121 (ImageNet pretrained)
       │
       ├── FROZEN ──► All Dense Blocks (backbone)
       │              (Preserve general visual features: edges, textures)
       │
       └── TRAINABLE ──► Classifier Head
                         FC(1024 → 8) + Sigmoid

  Optimizer  : Adam (lr = 1e-4)
  Epochs     : 10
  Batch Size : 32
  Loss       : Binary Cross-Entropy (multi-label)
  Scheduler  : ReduceLROnPlateau (factor=0.5, patience=3)
```

### Phase 2: Fine-Tuning

```
Objective: Domain-specific adaptation through selective unfreezing

  DenseNet121 (Phase 1 weights)
       │
       ├── STILL FROZEN ──► Dense Blocks 1 & 2 (low-level features)
       │
       └── TRAINABLE ──► Dense Block 3 + Dense Block 4 + Classifier
                         (High-level semantic features)

  Optimizer  : Adam (lr = 1e-5)     ← 10× lower than Phase 1
  Epochs     : 20
  Warmup     : Linear LR warm-up for first 3 epochs
  Scheduler  : Cosine Annealing LR
  Augmentation: RandomHorizontalFlip, RandomRotation(±10°), ColorJitter
```

> **Rationale**: Starting with a frozen backbone prevents catastrophic forgetting of ImageNet features during early training. Gradual unfreezing with a lower learning rate allows high-level features to specialize without distorting well-learned representations.

### FL Algorithms

#### FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017)

The foundational federated aggregation algorithm:

```
Algorithm: FedAvg
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Server initializes global model: w₀
2. For each communication round t = 1, 2, ..., T:
   a. Server broadcasts wₜ to all K clients
   b. Each client k computes:
      wₜ₊₁ᵏ = wₜ - η ∇L_k(wₜ)    [local SGD for E epochs]
   c. Server aggregates:
      wₜ₊₁ = Σ (nₖ / N) · wₜ₊₁ᵏ   [weighted average by data size]
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

#### FedProx (Li et al., 2020) — **Recommended for Heterogeneous Datasets**

FedProx extends FedAvg by adding a proximal regularization term to each client's local objective:

```
Algorithm: FedProx
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Client objective modified to:
  min_w  L_k(w) + (μ/2) ‖w - wₜ‖²

Where:
  L_k(w)          = local cross-entropy loss
  (μ/2)‖w - wₜ‖² = proximal term (pulls local model
                    toward global model)
  μ                = proximal coefficient (typically 0.01)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

#### Why FedProx Is Superior for This Task

| Property | FedAvg | FedProx |
|---|:---:|:---:|
| Handles statistical heterogeneity (non-IID data) | ❌ Poor | ✅ Designed for it |
| Convergence guarantee on heterogeneous data | ❌ Not guaranteed | ✅ Provably convergent |
| Prevents client drift | ❌ | ✅ Proximal term anchors local updates |
| Computation flexibility (partial updates) | ❌ | ✅ Supports dropping stragglers |
| Suitable for medical datasets with varying sizes | ❌ Limited | ✅ Excellent |

> **Critical Note**: CheXpert (~224K), NIH (~112K), and VinDr (~18K) have vastly different sizes and label distributions — a textbook case of *statistical heterogeneity*. FedProx's proximal term prevents the smaller VinDr client from drifting excessively from the global model.

## 📊 Statistical Analysis

### Why Statistical Validation Matters

In medical AI research, reporting only mean performance metrics is insufficient. Patient safety decisions require **statistically validated** improvements. Two models with 0.01 AUC difference may be statistically indistinguishable.

### Mean and Standard Deviation

All experiments are repeated **5 times** with different random seeds to account for training stochasticity:

| Model | AUC Mean | AUC Std Dev | 95% CI |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| DenseNet121 (baseline) | 0.832 | ±0.008 | [0.824, 0.840] |
| DenseNet+SE | 0.864 | ±0.006 | [0.858, 0.870] |
| FedProx+SE | 0.825 | ±0.005 | [0.820, 0.830] |
| FedProx+SE+FT | 0.831 | ±0.007 | [0.824, 0.838] |

### Paired t-Test

A **paired t-test** is applied to compare two models evaluated on the same test set across multiple runs:

```
Null Hypothesis (H₀): AUC(DenseNet+SE) = AUC(DenseNet baseline)
Alternative (H₁):     AUC(DenseNet+SE) > AUC(DenseNet baseline)

t-statistic = (mean_diff) / (std_diff / √n)
            = (0.032) / (0.005 / √5)
            = 14.31

p-value < 0.001  → Reject H₀ at α = 0.05
```

> ✅ The improvement from SE attention is **statistically significant** (p < 0.001), ruling out chance variation.

## 📉 Confusion Matrix

### What It Represents

A confusion matrix provides a **per-class diagnostic breakdown** of model predictions:

```
                     PREDICTED
                  Positive | Negative
                ┌──────────┬──────────┐
ACTUAL Positive │    TP    │    FN    │  ← Sensitivity = TP/(TP+FN)
                ├──────────┼──────────┤
       Negative │    FP    │    TN    │  ← Specificity = TN/(TN+FP)
                └──────────┴──────────┘
                     ↑
              Precision = TP/(TP+FP)
```

### Why It Is Useful in Medical AI

| Metric Derived | Clinical Importance |
|---|---|
| **Sensitivity (Recall)** | Fraction of actual disease cases correctly detected — minimizing false negatives is critical (missing a disease is dangerous) |
| **Specificity** | Fraction of healthy patients correctly identified — minimizing false positives reduces unnecessary procedures |
| **Precision (PPV)** | When the model predicts "disease", how often is it correct? |
| **F1-Score** | Harmonic mean of Precision and Recall — preferred when classes are imbalanced |

> In chest X-ray classification, **high recall is prioritized over precision** — a missed Pneumonia diagnosis is far more harmful than a false alarm.

## 📡 Communication Cost in Federated Learning

### Model Size and Transmission

| Component | Size |
|---|---|
| DenseNet121 weights | ~28 MB |
| SE Block additional weights | ~0.6 MB |
| **Total model size (DenseNet+SE)** | **~28.6 MB** |

### Communication Cost Formula

```
Total Communication Cost = Rounds × Model_Size × Clients × 2
                         (×2 for upload + download)

= 20 × 28.6 MB × 3 × 2
= 3,432 MB ≈ 3.35 GB per full FL experiment
```

### Per-Round Analysis

| Round Type | Data Transferred | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Server → Client (broadcast) | 28.6 MB × 3 clients = 85.8 MB | Downstream |
| Client → Server (aggregation) | 28.6 MB × 3 clients = 85.8 MB | Upstream |
| **Per Round Total** | **171.6 MB** | Both |
| **Full Experiment (20 rounds)** | **≈ 3.43 GB** | Total |

### Trade-offs

| Factor | FedAvg | FedProx |
|---|---|---|
| Communication per round | Identical | Identical |
| Convergence speed | Slower (more rounds needed) | Faster (proximal term stabilizes) |
| Total communication to convergence | Higher | **Lower** |
| Computation per round | Lower | Higher (proximal gradient) |

> 💡 **Practical note**: In real deployments, **model compression** (quantization, pruning) could reduce transmission cost by up to 4×. This remains a key area for future work.

## 🧠 Explainable AI (XAI)

### Grad-CAM: Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping

**Grad-CAM** (Selvaraju et al., 2017) generates class-discriminative heatmaps by leveraging gradients flowing into the final convolutional layer.

```
Grad-CAM Algorithm:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. Forward pass: Compute classification score S_c for class c
2. Gradient computation: ∂S_c / ∂A^k  (w.r.t. feature map A^k)
3. Global average pooling of gradients:
      α_k^c = (1/Z) Σᵢ Σⱼ (∂S_c / ∂A^k_ij)
4. Weighted combination of forward activation maps:
      L_Grad-CAM = ReLU(Σ_k α_k^c · A^k)
5. Upsample heatmap to input resolution (224×224)
6. Overlay on original X-ray (colormap: JET or PLASMA)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### How Heatmaps Work

The resulting heatmap highlights the **image regions that most influenced the model's prediction**:

| Heatmap Color | Activation Level | Meaning |
|:---:|:---:|---|
| 🔴 Red | High | Most discriminative region for predicted class |
| 🟡 Yellow | Medium | Moderately relevant region |
| 🔵 Blue | Low | Least influential region |

### Why Doctors Trust It

| Clinical Benefit | Description |
|---|---|
| **Spatial transparency** | Heatmap overlaid on the actual X-ray allows direct visual inspection |
| **Alignment validation** | Radiologists can verify if the model focuses on the correct anatomical region |
| **Error diagnosis** | When the model is wrong, Grad-CAM reveals if it was "looking at the wrong area" |
| **Regulatory compliance** | EU AI Act and FDA guidelines increasingly require explainability for medical AI |
| **Training tool** | Medical students can compare model attention to expert attention |

> 🩺 On **VinDr-CXR** (radiologist-annotated), Grad-CAM activations are validated against clinician-provided bounding box annotations — confirming that the model focuses on pathologically relevant regions.

## ⚠️ Limitations

No research is without limitations. We acknowledge the following honestly:

| Limitation | Impact | Mitigation Explored |
|---|---|---|
| **Dataset Label Noise** | CheXpert and NIH labels are NLP-extracted — systematic errors may bias training | Label smoothing, uncertainty weighting |
| **Severe Class Imbalance** | Rare conditions (Pneumothorax) have far fewer positive samples, biasing metrics | Weighted BCE loss, oversampling |
| **Simulated Federation** | Federated clients run on a single machine — real network latency and communication failures not modeled | Planned real distributed testing |
| **2D Imaging Limitation** | Chest X-rays lack the depth information of CT scans — some pathologies are ambiguous in 2D | Out of scope for this work |
| **Computational Cost** | DenseNet+SE+FedProx training is expensive (~48 GPU-hours for full experiment) | Gradient checkpointing, mixed precision |
| **Limited FL Clients** | Only 3 federated clients — real-world hospital networks may involve 50+ institutions | Scalability experiments planned |

## 🚀 Future Work

This research opens multiple compelling directions for future investigation:

### 🔹 Short-Term (6–12 Months)

- **Personalized Federated Learning**: Implement per-FTML (Federated Meta-Learning) and DITTO algorithms to allow each client to maintain a personalized local model adapted to its own distribution, while still benefiting from global federated training.

- **Label Noise Robustness**: Integrate robust loss functions (Symmetric Cross-Entropy, GCE) and label noise detection mechanisms to reduce the impact of NLP-label uncertainty in CheXpert and NIH.

### 🔹 Medium-Term (12–24 Months)

- **Vision Transformer (ViT) Backbone**: Replace DenseNet121 with **DeiT** or **Swin Transformer** architectures, which have demonstrated superior performance on medical imaging tasks while providing token-level attention visualization as a natural explainability mechanism.

- **Self-Supervised Pre-Training**: Leverage contrastive learning (SimCLR, MoCo) on unlabeled X-ray data as a domain-specific pretraining step, reducing dependence on ImageNet initialization and improving cross-domain transfer.

### 🔹 Long-Term (24+ Months)

- **Multimodal Learning with MIMIC-CXR**: Integrate **radiology report text** (via ClinicalBERT or BioMedBERT) with image features to build vision-language models that simultaneously learn from visual and textual clinical evidence.

- **Differential Privacy Integration**: Augment the FL pipeline with **DP-SGD** (differentially private stochastic gradient descent) to provide formal privacy guarantees — moving from privacy-by-architecture (FL) to privacy-by-proof.

- **Federated Continual Learning**: Enable models to learn from new client data over time without forgetting previous knowledge — critical for a healthcare system where new hospitals continuously join the federated network.
